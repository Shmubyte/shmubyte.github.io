Common Types of Surveillance

Digital and Internet Surveillance:

  1. Internet traffic monitoring – tracking data packets, websites visited, and online behavior.
  2. Social media monitoring – analyzing posts, likes, follows, and connections.
  3. Email and messaging surveillance – scanning communications for keywords, attachments, or metadata.
  4. Search engine surveillance – collecting and analyzing search queries for patterns or interests.
  5. ISP (Internet Service Provider) surveillance – tracking what users access online, often for data retention or government compliance.
  6. Phone tapping – Pegasus, and Graphite by paragon. Intercepting voice calls or text messages.
  7. Wi-Fi sniffing – capturing unencrypted data over wireless networks.

Biometric Surveillance

  1. Fingerprint and palm scanners
  2. Facial and iris recognition- As used in cell-phones and devices like Amazon's Alexa.
  3. Voice recognition- As used in openAI's ChatGPT or Alexa.
  4. Gait analysis – identifying people by their movement patterns.
  5. DNA databases – used for law enforcement or border security.

Location and Movement Surveillance

  1. Drone Camera footage - The DHS flew surveillance drones over the LA protests recently.
  2. GPS tracking – via smartphones, vehicles, or wearable devices.
  3. License plate readers (ALPRs) – As used by Flock Safety in conjucnction with law enforcement: Automatically scan and log vehicle plates.
  4. Smart city sensors – monitor traffic flow, foot traffic, or crowd density.
  5. RFID and Bluetooth tracking – used in workplaces, retail, or transit systems.

Data and Algorithmic Surveillance

  1. Data mining and profiling – extracting patterns from user data.
  2. Predictive policing algorithms – forecast crime-prone areas or individuals.
  3. Credit scoring and risk assessment – track financial and behavioral data.
  4. Behavioral analytics – used by tech platforms to infer habits, preferences, or emotions.

Physical and Visual Surveillance

  1. CCTV (Closed-Circuit Television) – continuous or motion-triggered recording in public or private spaces.
  2. Body cameras – worn by police or security personnel.
  3. Facial recognition systems – match faces to databases for identification.
  4. Drone surveillance – airborne monitoring over large areas.
  5. Thermal and infrared imaging – detect body heat and movements, even through walls or in darkness.
